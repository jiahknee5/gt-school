How to use

The lens — the real problem
Cue

S52:30–4:00 · Backbone
Why the numbers are real
Cue

Slide 5. Trace the payment flow, then the three cards.

Say

“Why can you trust these numbers? Three reasons. First, the programs are isolated at the database, not by application code — Summer Camp's P&L physically can't bleed into Fall's, and a family enrolled in both is counted correctly in each. A query in the wrong scope returns zero rows; a cross-program write is rejected outright. Second, payments are idempotent: Stripe can retry a webhook and the deposit still counts once, so your pipeline and your revenue are real, not inflated. Third, when Supabase — which owns funnel and income — disagrees with HubSpot, the number wears a data-confidence warning instead of quietly lying to you. And every figure traces to exactly one source, so a number means the same thing on every screen. Phase 2 spends this backbone; it doesn't re-solve it.”

S64:00–4:40 · Data
The data behind the dashboards
Cue

Slide 6. Point at the channel figures, then the real/stood-in table.

Say

“The dashboards aren't lorem-ipsum. They sit on twelve hundred families modeled to GT's real funnel — and the channel mix encodes what a marketer would recognize: X converts around 45%, Facebook is a volume trap at 8%, and a lead who's already clicked converts at 53% versus 11% cold. There are fifteen deliberate edge cases — duplicate payments, a family in two programs, conflicting records, missing UTM — so the isolation and the reconciliation are provable against data I built to break them. And what's real — HubSpot, Stripe, Supabase, Open Data — versus what's stood-in — Meta, GA4, summer.gt — is labeled, row by row. The brief scores how I think about data, not just how I wire it.”

S85:10–7:00 · Demo
A campaign, end to end
Cue

Slide 8. Walk the flow; this is the handoff into the live demo if recording.

Say

“Let me show one campaign end to end, because it exercises every theme at once. The GT Challenge is a gifted-style quiz GT runs on social. A parent sees the post, their child takes it, we capture a consented lead and return a fit result — never a pass-fail on a child — and the program routes strong fits into admissions. To marketing, it reads as a channel: $8,208 of spend, twelve qualified leads, a cost-per-qualified-lead of $684 — measured, not assumed — sitting beside every other channel, and that spend rolls into the same $365K budget. Open Data sharpens the call: in lower-rated Austin and Dallas districts the qualified-lead cost is about $41 versus $63 blended, which flips a pilot decision to approve. That's capture, assessment, reconciliation, and reporting — on the same backbone, nothing double-counted.”

Marketing-first voice — quick reminders

Lead with the outcome, back it with the mechanism. “Clean per-program revenue” → because RLS isolation. Not the reverse.

Numbers are marketing numbers: CAC, CPQL, funnel stage, speed-to-lead, budget variance, days-to-cutoff. The engineering is why you can trust them.

Cut hedges and hype. No “AI-powered,” “seamless,” “robust.” Say the specific thing: “$684, measured” / “rejected by the database” / “says stand-in.”

Handoff: end Slide 8 (or 9) by switching to the live app for the demo — this deck is the framing; the walkthrough script carries the clicks.
